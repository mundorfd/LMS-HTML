What is Company Culture?

Culture affects every aspect of your company, from the public's perception of your brand to your own job satisfaction to the bottom line. It's important that your company culture is adaptable and open to improvement. This starts with being able to articulate just what kind of culture your company has.

As you scan the types of cultures and examples below, you begin to notice one thing: Often the culture affects the engineering and vice-versa. The architecture, the technologies, the tools. the delivery model, the feedback loop, the freedom to innovate: All of these are part of the culture. And all can make a difference in whether employees will be a good fit or not.

The 5 Types of Company Cultures

No two cultures are exactly alike, but there are defining characteristics that tend to place organizational cultures into five different types.

Team-first Company Culture

Team-oriented companies hire for culture fit first, skills and experience second.

At Team-oriented companies, employee engagement is the top priority. These companies may hold frequent team outings, opportunities to provide meaningful feedback and flexibility to accommodate employees' family.

Service-oriented companies often embrace Team-first company culture. This type of culture normally makes for happy employees, and happy employees will give better customer service.

Netflix is a great example – they offer unlimited family leave and vacation days.

Elite Corporate Culture

Companies with elite cultures are out to change the world.

An elite corporate culture hires only the best and brightest. Elite companies are pushing the envelope and need employees to not merely keep up, but lead the way (think Google). These are innovative and daring companies who hire creative, confident, capable, and competitive candidates.

SpaceX is a high-profile example of an innovative company doing big things in aerospace manufacturing and space transport. Expectations are high and 60 to 70-hour work weeks are the norm. Knowing that they're doing meaningful, history-making work keeps most employees motivated.

Horizontal Corporate Culture

Titles don't mean much in horizontal cultures.

Horizontal corporate culture is common among startups. This type of culture makes for a collaborative, everyone-pitch-in mindset. Companies that embrace the Horizontal culture are typically younger and smaller and have the ability to be flexible and able to change based on research or feedback. They do whatever they can to keep the customer happy—their success depends on it.

Basecamp is the perfect example of a successful company that maintains a startup-like mindset. Originally founded as 37Signals, Basecamp announced last year that it would focus exclusively on its most popular product and maintain its relative small size rather than grow into something much bigger and broader.

Conventional Corporate Culture

Traditional companies have clearly defined hierarchies and are still grappling with the learning curve for communicating through new mediums.

Traditional companies will have clearly defined hierarchies. They may have a dress code (tie, slacks). They normally have a numbers-focused approach and favor risk-averse decision making. The customer is not necessarily always right—the bottom line takes precedence.

General Electric is one example. GE is about as traditional as they come and is well-known for its cut-and-dry management practices.

Progressive Corporate Culture

Uncertainty is the definitive trait of a transitional culture

Mergers, acquisitions or sudden changes in the market can all contribute to a progressive culture. Companies often have investors to answer to as well as employees. In this kind of environment, anxious employees often don't know what to expect next. Keep in mind, however, that while change can be scary, it can also be good.

Amazon's $13.7 billion acquisition of Whole Foods is one fairly recent example of companies in transition. Amazon's data-driven culture clashed with Whole Food's culture of empowerment, causing headaches for Whole Food's employees.

Some Examples

Netflix

Netflix has one of the most enviable engineering cultures in the software industry. Ever hear of microservices? Netflix invented them.

Microservices changed the engineering culture at Netflix. Today, every developer runs what they write (often a microservice). They're responsible for fixing their tools if they break, writing the documentation, and dealing with any operations pain they create.

Themes

Freedom and responsibility are at the core of Netflix's business strategy. They attract talent by giving employees the freedom to be creative and flexible, but they only hire responsible people who are worthy of that freedom.

Facebook

Facebook's employee handbook emphasizes that they do not want to be beaten by competitors. Facebook stresses the importance of releasing fast, failing fast, and learning fast.

Management believes the industry is moving so fast that there's no point in having a five-year plan. Instead, they have a six-month plan and a constantly shifting 30-year plan. The handbook states that there are approximately one engineer for every one million users on Facebook. Which is amazing when you think about it.

Themes

You get the sense of a healthy paranoia at Facebook when you read some of the other passages: "If we don't create the thing that kills Facebook, someone else will." There's also an aura of tough love: "Greatness and comfort rarely coexist,". Greatness, not money, seems to be the goal because Facebook says it makes money so that it can build better services. It doesn't build services to make money.

Money quote:

"Fast doesn't just win the race. It gets a head start for the next one. "

Etsy

The online arts and crafts marketplace has a very different application architecture compared with Netflix's microservices. Etsy is a monolithic service-oriented architecture with a disciplined continuous delivery process and a complete feedback loop of about 21 minutes! Its engineers deploy code about 25 times a day, and that gives developers the feeling that their work is making an impact.

Etsy's culture cites this quotation from Dan Ariely's book, The Upside of Irrationality: The Unexpected Benefits of Defying Logic at Work and at Home.

"If companies really want their workers to produce, they should try to impart a sense of meaning—not just through vision statements but by allowing employees to feel a sense of completion and ensuring that a job well done is acknowledged. At the end of the day, such factors can exert a huge influence on satisfaction and productivity. "

Like other companies on this list, Etsy has a relatively flat organizational structure. In fact, they say that their company operates internally like an online community. They are both self-regulating and self-sustaining. Workers (especially developers and operations) are encouraged to "watch each other's back."

Themes

Etsy believes engineering processes that make its developers happy are also best for product quality. "Easy deploys = developer happiness," is the motto. If employees break something later in their tenure, it's okay—they do blameless post-mortems.
