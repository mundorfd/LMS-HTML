Key Concepts

At this point, you should be able to answer all the following questions:

  1. How do the classic Diffie–Hellman and X25519 key-exchange routines compute a shared secret, and why does the discrete-logarithm problem protect them?
  2. What attack can a man-in-the-middle mount against unauthenticated DH, and why does passive eavesdropping alone fail?
  3. How do digital signatures, pre-shared keys, and certificate-based authentication (SIGMA, TLS) bind identities to DH values?
  4. In what order do the TLS 1.3 key-schedule secrets (Early, Handshake, Master, Application) arise, and which inputs feed each stage?
  5. What distinguishes the various Noise handshake patterns (NN, XX, IK) in terms of authentication timing, forward secrecy, and resistance to key-compromise impersonation?
  6. Why does forward secrecy ensure that even if long-term keys leak, previously recorded sessions remain safe?

If any of these remain unclear, please revisit the module pages and post questions on the ED Discussion board. Mastering these outcomes will prepare you for hybrid and post-quantum key-exchange topics in the next module.
